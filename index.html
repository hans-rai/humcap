UNFPA Bangladesh Humanitarian Evaluation

Assessment of Capacity in Humanitarian Action (2025) - Recommendations Overview

A
Develop Nexus Guidance Note
Country Level
A. UNFPA Bangladesh should develop a guidance note on the core elements of working at the nexus, in order to better operationalize its commitments to bridging the humanitarian-development divide as articulated in the 10th CPD. This note should address the challenges and possibilities of government sustainability of humanitarian response programming.
Linked Conclusions:
Conclusion 1: "UNFPA in Bangladesh is committed to investing in climate adaptation and working across the nexus, and is spearheading many important initiatives that facilitate preparedness, early disaster response, resilient early recovery, and, in Cox's Bazar, area-based responses that include host communities. The current CPD underscores this commitment, as does UNFPA's innovative programming such as cash and voucher assistance, anticipatory action and decentralized procurement. However, there is no specific articulation in the CPD or in specific country programme guidance on what the nexus entails for the CO."

Conclusion 4: "UNFPA support has led to significant increases in utilization of a range of SRH, GBV and A&Y services in Cox's Bazar and to a lesser, but still important, extent in sudden-onset disasters over the evaluation period. UNFPA has also improved the quality of these services over time to better address the needs of specific populations, such as PWD and third gender populations. Moreover, UNFPA has sought to maximize efficiency in delivery of services through integrated approaches. However, despite UNFPA's strong relationship with the Government of Bangladesh, sustainability of services presents ongoing and future challenges, particularly in relation to accelerating funding shortfalls affecting Cox's Bazar and short project cycles for disasters."

1
EQ1: Relevance/Appropriateness
While the current (10th) CPD for Bangladesh (2022-2026) departs from the 9th CPD's humanitarian commitments insofar as it does not reference refugee response, there is significantly increased attention to disaster preparedness and response and UNFPA's commitment to working across the humanitarian-development continuum in the 10th CPD.
Rationale & Evidence
Cox's Bazar Context:
The 10th CPD explicitly excludes Rohingya response, directing it to the Joint Response Plan, while increasing focus on disaster preparedness.
Country Office Context:
Key informants reported the "humanitarian-development continuum" is not specifically defined in the 10th CPD document or country guidance, creating uncertainty about practical implementation targets. Several UNFPA key informants expressed concern that the meaning is not specifically defined, leading to unclear targets for nexus work in Bangladesh.
Key Evidence:
"Several key informants from UNFPA expressed concern that the meaning of the humanitarian-development continuum is not specifically defined within the 10th CPD document, nor elsewhere in country guidance."
8
EQ4: Data for Response
UNFPA is facilitating improved use and dissemination of reliable programme and population data in humanitarian response in Bangladesh, although further efforts could show how UNFPA support positively impacts the lives of women and girls, and communities more broadly.
Rationale & Evidence
Cox's Bazar Context:
UNFPA's investment in data gathering in Cox's Bazar response (from information management systems, programme monitoring to specialized evaluations) has contributed to its ability to advocate for continued, evidence-based interventions within the JRPs.
Country Office Context:
While data is used for strategic decisions, there's a gap in demonstrating how interventions translate to positive impact on communities. UNFPA utilizes monitoring systems including analyzing real-time data on service uptake to identify areas with low uptake and make programmatic adjustments.
Key Evidence:
"In just one example provided by a UNFPA key informant, the analysis of family planning service uptake in the camps has helped determine that service distribution is working, leading to its continuation and informing resource requests."
11
EQ6: Efficiency
UNFPA Bangladesh has implemented several procedural improvements and innovations to facilitate humanitarian response, with key support from the UNFPA AP regional office.
Rationale & Evidence
Country Office Context:
A significant achievement was the finalization in 2023 of country-level SOPs that guide the CO in its initial response to natural disasters affecting the country. These SOPs outline key steps, budget, emergency funding processes, timelines, roles, and responsibilities for both preparedness and response.
Key Evidence:
"According to a UNFPA key informant, their implementation has reduced response time significantly, from nearly three months after a disaster to three days, which IP key informants also noted."
Additional Innovation:
"The CO has also adapted regional guidance on AAP to Bangladesh context to make it more operational and finalized an Anticipatory Action protocol in 2024."
13
EQ6: Efficiency
Although well-staffed in comparison to other UNFPA COs, ensuring adequate human resources has been a challenge in both Cox's Bazar and in UNFPA's disaster response.
Rationale & Evidence
Cox's Bazar Context:
In the past there have been leadership vacuums and severe staffing reductions in key areas like GBV coordination, impacting agency presence, strength and advocacy capacity. Staff were on temporary appointments or service contracts.
Country Office Context:
Key informants expressed that the scope and frequency of disasters in Bangladesh require a more substantial team of humanitarian-dedicated staff than has existed to date. One UNFPA key informant expressed the view that it is "time to make a full-face humanitarian team for Bangladesh country office."
Key Evidence:
"A human resources realignment took place around mid-2023... to transition a large number of Cox's Bazar staff to fixed-term contracts instead of temporary appointments or service contracts. UNFPA Cox's Bazar managed to complete a 'remarkable' 40 recruitments in one year as part of this realignment."
16
EQ8: Connectedness
UNFPA promotes several humanitarian preparedness and response interventions in Bangladesh that support, and plan for, longer-term developmental and resilience-related goals.
Rationale & Evidence
Cox's Bazar Context:
UNFPA is considering approaches for transitioning towards more area-based responses, recognizing the overlap between humanitarian and development needs given that the Rohingya have been in Bangladesh for eight years.
Country Office Context:
Programmatically, UNFPA implements initiatives that are integrated with the HDP nexus. Examples include provision of conditional cash support for adolescent girls to stay in school after disasters, specifically aimed at preventing child marriage, a harmful norm often exacerbated by crises.
Key Evidence:
"UNFPA has engaged in discussions with and received funding from International Financial Institutions such as the World Bank and Asian Development Bank, recognizing the potential for development funding to support resilience activities for both host and refugee populations."
Specific Example:
"UNFPA support to MHPSS in emergency settings also contributes to the resilience and well-being of affected populations. UNFPA has also drawn from its emergency work—such as support to the national hotline in emergency response—to build out the hotline response in development."
B
Explore Diverse Funding Streams
Country Level
B. UNFPA should continue its innovative work to explore different funding streams for emergencies, including through anticipatory action and engagement with IFIs. As part of this, UNFPA must be able to illustrate clearly the life-saving nature of its work (see recommendation C). UNFPA Bangladesh should document successes for wider agency learning.
Linked Conclusion:
Conclusion 2: "UNFPA is widely recognized as a key UN partner in the Rohingya refugee response and in response to sudden-onset natural disasters in Bangladesh. Its facilitation of SRH, GBV, and A&Y coordination and programming has positioned UNFPA as a leader in these sectors. However, funding for these sectors remains consistently lower than other humanitarian sectors. This presents significant challenges to coverage of needs, particularly in disaster response. UNFPA has used and continues to diversify funding mechanisms to expand coverage, such as through IFI funding in Cox's Bazar."

EQ1: Relevance/Appropriateness
UNFPA mandate areas are integrated within the annual Joint Response Plans for the Rohingya refugee crisis as well as in appeals documents for disaster response, including CERF appeals for anticipatory action funding.
Rationale & Evidence
Cox's Bazar Context:
In the Rohingya response, UNFPA participates in the annual JRP process, which is led by the ISCG in Cox's Bazar and is based on joint needs assessments. UNFPA's leadership in coordination mechanisms for SRH, GBV and youth has contributed to its ability to ensure attention to its mandate areas in the JRP.
Country Office Context:
UNFPA's mandate areas are systematically integrated into Humanitarian Response Plans (HRPs), Humanitarian Needs Overviews (HNOs), and appeals for funding. This integration is facilitated by UNFPA's strategic positioning within the disaster response architecture as a member of the HCTT.
Key Evidence:
"UNFPA also participates in the Anticipatory Action Technical Working Group (AATWB)... Several external key informants noted the importance of UNFPA's presence in this working group and the agency's significant success in ensuring attention to UNFPA's mandate areas are included in CERF funding for anticipatory action."
Funding Data:
"Financial tracking for health more generally and for Protection and GBV across the years covered in the review confirm that funding has been lower than for other sectors."
6
EQ3: GBV Effectiveness
UNFPA and partners have made notable and ongoing contributions to the quality and effectiveness of GBV response in Bangladesh's humanitarian crises, although coverage is an increasingly urgent challenge.
Rationale & Evidence
Cox's Bazar Context:
UNFPA has led the GBV Sub-Sector since 2017, supporting delivery of GBV services through 21 UNFPA-supported health facilities and 55 women-friendly spaces across 33 camps. The focus gradually moved from setting up services to expanding services and monitoring and improving quality of care.
Country Office Context:
At the national level for disaster response, UNFPA co-leads the national GBV Cluster with MOWCA. This leadership has been instrumental in ensuring GBV is recognized as a life-saving intervention in humanitarian action in Bangladesh.
Funding Impact Example:
"The most recent GBVIMS (Q1 2025) Bulletin notes that the US Government funding freeze had a profound effect on GBV actors, leading to disruptions in service delivery across eight camps and two host communities: Women and Girls' Safe Spaces in Camps 5, 13, 14, 15, 16, 18, 22, 25, and other GBV service points were either closed or operated on reduced schedules."
Innovation Example:
"UNFPA in Cox's Bazar previously worked with the World Bank on an integrated GBV and SRH project that served both refugees and host communities... This new project has a significant split (almost 50/50) of funding between Rohingya refugees and host communities."
7
EQ3: GBV Effectiveness
Uptake of GBV services in humanitarian crises in Bangladesh has improved, particularly in the refugee response, but also for disaster-affected populations. However, data on the impact of services on survivors, their families and communities are lacking.
Rationale & Evidence
Cox's Bazar Context:
There has been a significant increase in the willingness to report GBV among the Rohingya population, increasing from 14% of women expressing willingness to report in 2018 to 85% in 2024, according to self-reports and survey respondents.
Country Office Context:
Community engagement and mobilization efforts have made communities more "responsive to take quality care from the government facility." Mobile services are reaching excluded communities who are accessing integrated SRH/GBV services for the first time.
Impact Data Gap:
"IPs more often report on activities and numbers reached, rather than behavioral change or other impact indicators, such as women's decreased risk of violence. The absence of baseline or endline evaluations further inhibits the ability of UNFPA and its IPs to measure change."

EQ5: Leadership
For the refugee response, UNFPA has overall shown strong leadership in GBV, SRH, and youth coordination, building out capacity and partnership, and addressing challenges effectively as they arise.
Rationale & Evidence
Cox's Bazar Context:
UNFPA has taken a clear leadership role in GBV, SRH and youth coordination, with many positive results. In the words of one interviewee, UNFPA is a "very critical part of the overall coordination network" in the refugee response.
GBV Coordination Example:
"When a camp authority tried to enter and lock a WFS, the GBV SS took the lead, communicated with the Refugee, Relief and Repatriation Commissioner (RRRC), and organized a coordination meeting with Camp-in-Charges (CICs) to discuss appropriate protocols."
SRH Coordination Success:
"The SRH WG, under UNFPA's coordination, launched the GoB Family Planning Strategy for Rohingya Refugees (2022-2025) in collaboration with the Directorate General of Family Planning (DGFP), in 2022."

EQ5: Leadership
For disaster responses, GBV coordination is widely recognized as effective. SRH coordination has faced more (external) challenges, but it is also functioning effectively. A&Y do not have a separate coordination mechanism; they utilize an integrated approach.
Rationale & Evidence
Country Office Context:
Through its leadership of the GBV Cluster, in partnership with the MOWCA, UNFPA has had a "very strong voice" in establishing GBV coordination for disaster response. GBV protection issues were not widely considered important in disaster response before the introduction of the GBV Cluster in 2016.
SRH Coordination Challenge:
"Challenges existed with bringing attention to SRH in disasters because the Health Cluster sits under the Infectious Disease Control Department of MOH. Initially, SRH had not been prioritized by Health Cluster leadership because their orientation was towards disease outbreaks."
Youth Coordination Approach:
"Unlike in Cox's Bazar, there is no specific A&Y coordination mechanism at the national level for disasters. However, UNFPA has significantly grown its A&Y programming since 2022 with a focus on institutionalization and strengthened partnership with government entities."
C
Enhance Data Systems & Impact Measurement
Country Level
C. UNFPA Bangladesh should consider ways in which its population dynamics experts can scale up their contributions to developing better systems for data collection in emergencies, such as designing scalable pre-disaster assessments directly with affected populations; more standardized approaches to illustrating the life-saving impact of GBV interventions; and updating the MISP calculator for Bangladesh.
Linked Conclusion:
Conclusion 3: "UNFPA plays a crucial role in data collection in humanitarian response, from assessments to targeted surveys to information management to programme monitoring, and utilizes data to support the wider humanitarian community and to improve UNFPA's own programmatic response. However, UNFPA's data experts (including population data staff) are not mobilized to their full capacity to support data collection and management for disaster preparedness and response. This includes pre-disaster consultations with affected populations, developing a common beneficiary database for anticipatory action (and joint activities with other agencies), and with the MISP calculator, which requires updating. Data collection could also focus more strategically on capturing the impact of services, in order to better illustrate the life-saving nature of UNFPA-supported interventions, particularly related to GBV."

UNFPA is involved in and leads a variety of needs assessments that promote engagement of marginalized groups and support access to services for specific marginalized populations. However, an awareness of needs does not always translate into adequate response, particularly in disasters.
Rationale & Evidence
Cox's Bazar Context:
In Cox's Bazar in 2024, UNFPA reviewed questions and indicators in the Inter Sector Needs Assessment (ISNA) related to GBV, SRH and youth, and supported training for the ISNA enumerators to ensure they understood the assessment questions and their research approach was safe and ethical.
Country Office Context:
Typically in disasters, UNFPA uses preexisting SRH data, including with the MISP calculator, to assess needs when a disaster strikes. However, two external key informants noted that the MISP calculator is based on outdated census data.
Data Limitation:
"The MISP calculator that we are still using is based on the last census. Maybe they can update it based on the recent census data, so that the accuracy level will be higher." - UNFPA IP Key Informant
Coverage Challenge:
"Resource limitations mean UNFPA can often only reach a minimal percentage (according to one interviewee, perhaps 10-20%) of the affected population, even when needs assessments clearly show a significantly larger scale of impact."

EQ2: SRH Effectiveness
Utilization of a broad range of quality SRH services has improved in Cox's Bazar and to a lesser (but still important) extent in acute disasters during the review period.
Rationale & Evidence
Cox's Bazar Context:
An SRH survey undertaken in 2024 showed that contraceptive prevalence rates (CPR) increased significantly from almost 33% in 2021 to 55% in 2024 among the Rohingyas. Adolescent pregnancies among the Rohingya population fell from 18% in 2019 to 7% in 2024.
Country Office Context:
Data from the SRH response to flash floods in 2022 suggests that a total of 1,213 safe deliveries were conducted by skilled midwives during the 9 months of the project period--a significant increase compared to only 120 deliveries in the same facilities in the 9 months prior to the intervention.
Impact Data:
"While maternal deaths in the refugee camps remain a major challenge, there has been a gradual decline in the number of deaths, from 84 in 2021 to 46 in 2024. This is linked to efforts including the maternal and perinatal mortality surveillance system and expert review committees."

EQ7: Coherence
UNFPA has mainstreamed co-location and integration of GBV, SRH and youth services as a standard approach in both refugee response and disasters to build synergies and maximize resources.
Rationale & Evidence
Cox's Bazar Context:
Examples of integration include: GBV case workers placed in SRH facilities supported by UNFPA; SRH midwives placed in WFS and WLCCs which are GBV-focused facilities; SRH services available in youth centers; all UNFPA's centers provide referral to its other programmes.
Country Office Context:
Examples from disaster response include: Mobile SRH Health Camps used during disasters provide referrals for GBV response services; Union-level health facilities supported to provide life-saving SRH and health sector response to GBV services through midwives.
Integration Benefits:
"UNFPA documentation regularly emphasizes the benefits of this integration, in terms of ensuring needs of women and girls are met more comprehensively; programmes are mutually supportive; and entry points for women and girls to access services are amplified."

EQ8: Connectedness
While UNFPA's humanitarian interventions often include capacity development and ownership at national and local levels, short-term and limited funding affects success in this area.
Rationale & Evidence
Cox's Bazar Context:
UNFPA prioritizes multi-year funding as is possible, particularly in Cox's Bazar where the majority of UNFPA's 48-50 IPs are local. Training of youth volunteers to manage youth centers in the Rohingya camps is another example of building out localization efforts.
Country Office Context:
A key example of government ownership is the integration of the MISP into the government operational plan, which covers both development and disasters. UNFPA trains midwives in collaboration with the government and deploys them to government facilities.
Sustainability Challenge:
"When asked about the sustainability of programming initiated for disaster response, a UNFPA key informant noted: 'The counselor who deployed to the government facility, I would say that is not fully sustainable, because whenever any disaster ends they lose funding.'"

Global Context:
The Bangladesh CO faces challenges with a lack of dedicated humanitarian M&E staff and uses basic tools like Google Sheets, expressing a need for a central software-based database. This is a systemic resourcing and tools issue that requires a global solution.
Systemic Solution Needed:
"HRD can support the development and rollout of corporate monitoring systems and software for emergencies. The current system for humanitarian response uses basic tools like google sheets to store data, not a dedicated humanitarian database."

EQ7: Coherence
UNFPA harmonizes humanitarian response activities with sister agencies via a number of useful channels, although in disaster response resolution on the common beneficiary database has impacted coordination on service delivery for AA.
Rationale & Evidence
Global Context:
The bottleneck in creating a common beneficiary database for Anticipatory Action is partly due to a lack of global standards and data-sharing protocols. The Bangladesh CO has been communicating with HQ on this need.
Corporate Standards Needed:
"HRD, in coordination with other agencies at the global level, is the appropriate entity to develop corporate data-sharing agreements and technical standards for beneficiary databases to enable effective inter-agency collaboration."
HQ Communication:
"It was noted by a UNFPA key informant that (a) CO Bangladesh has been communicating to HQs on the need to create corporate beneficiary database, and (b) HRD has not necessarily been proactive in this regard."
